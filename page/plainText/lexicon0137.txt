Dedicatorie
TO THE MOST
high, and mightie prince,
ELIZABETH, by the grace
of God, of England, Fraunce, and
Ireland, Queene: Defen­
der of the faith
etc.

CIcero did ac­
count it wor­
thie his labor,
and no les pro­
fitable to the Roman com­
mon weale (Most gratious
Soueraigne,) to inuent a
speedie kinde of wryting
by Character, a Plu­







tarch reporteth in the life
of CATO the yonger. This
inuention was increased
afterward by Seneca: that
the number of Characters
grue to 7000. Whether
through iniurie of time, or
that men gaue it ouer for
tediousnes of learning, no­
thing remaineth extant of
Ciceros inuention at this
day. Vpon consideration
of the great vse of such a
kinde of writing, I haue
inuented the like: of fewe
Characters, short, & easie,
euery Character answe­







ring a word: My inuenti­
on meere English, without
precept, or imitation of a­
ny. The vses are diuers:
Short, that a swifte hande
may therewith write Ora­
tions or publike actions of
speach, vttered as becom­
eth the grauitie of such
actions, verbatim. Se­
crete, as no kinde of wry­
ting like, and herein (be­
sides other properties) ex­
celling the writing by let­
ters, and Alphabet, in that,
Nations of strange langua­
ges, may hereby communi­







cate their meaning toge­
ther in writing, though of
sundrie tonges. It is repor­
ted of the people of Chi­
na, that they haue no o­
ther kinde, and so traf­
fike together many Pro­
uinces of that kingdom, ig­
norant one of an others
speach. Their Characters
are very long, and harde
to make, that a dousen of
mine, may be written as
soone as one of theirs; Be­
sides, they wanting an Al­
phabet, fal into an infinite
number, which is a thing







that greatlie chargeth me­
mory, and may discourage
the learner.

This my inuention I
am emboldned to dedicate
vnto your Maiesty, in that
among other your Prince­
lie vertues, your Maiesty
is woont to approue of eue­
rie good and profitable in­
uention of learning: and
in duetie of thankefulnesse
am I much more bounde
thereunto, from whome
I haue receiued assurance
of the fruite of my stu­
dies, by your Maiesties







most gracious Priuiledge.
And this my inuention be­
ing altogether of English
yeeld, where your Maiestie
is the Ladie of the Soyle it
appertayneth of right to
you onely. So, mooued by
duetie, and incouraged by
your Maiesties fauoura­
ble disposition to the ver­
tuous, & learned indeuors
of your faithfull Subiects,
I haue presumed to publish
my Charactery vnder the
protection of your Maie­
sties name. It is like a ten­
der plant, yong & strange







and so it resteth.

If it may be so happy, as
to inioye the influence of
your Maiesties fauoure,
and good liking, I doubt
not, but it will growe vp,
be embraced, & yeeld pro­
fitable fruit vnto many,
and I my selfe thereby shal
haue atteined for my par&shyl
ticular respect, that which
in a lower degree, many
shal enioy by the vse of this
my inuention, which I hope
(be it said with modestie)
wanteth little to equall
it, with that olde devise







of Ciceroes, but your Ma­
iesties alowance, & Cice­
roes name. The later as I

the former my Characterie
dareth presume no farther,
but liueth, or dieth, accor­
ding to your Maiesties ac­
count, whose blessed state,
as it is to all your loyall
subiectes an other life, be­
sides the naturall, so to this
new sprong ympe, & to me
the parent therof, nothing
can bee more comfortable
then your Maiesties grati­
ous acceptation, by whom







all the land flourisheth, & is
at the very heart cheered.

The eternall blesse your
Maiestie with increase of
all happinesse to your com­

Your Maiesties faithful

Timothe Bright.















An Instruction to the
Reader, how the art is
to be learned

THou hast here gen­
tle Reader, an Art
of short, and so of
speedie wryting,
plainly deliuered vnto thee.
So as by thine owne industry,
thou mayest attaine vnto it, if
thou wilt but one moneth
take paines therein, & by con­
tinuance of an other moneth,
mayest thou attaine to great
readinesse. For thy better in­
strucion, thou art first to learne
the Characterie wordes by
heart, and therewith the ma­
king of the figure to the Cha­
racter, to doo it readily, and
cleane. Then, to be able to
joyne euery Character to the







worde pronounced, without
booke or sight of any patterne
before thee. This done, thou
art farther to proceede, and to
learne how to referre eyther
wordes of like signification, or
of the same kinde, or contra­
ries vnto those that be called
Characterie. Here because e­
uery man by his own reache
can not consider how to refer
all wordes, thou hast in this
booke an English dictionary,
with words of reference alrea­
dy thereto adioyned to helpe
such as of themselues can not so
dispose them. The words which
are called appellatiue, if they
signifie thinges that haue di­
stinct parts: those parts are to
be written in the one side, and
the things and all the sortes
that kinde, on the other, and







where there be many of a kind
for cleerer distinction, part them
vnto diuerse sides. Likewise, as
thou maiest see in the table of
them more particularly. In stead
of much laborious writing; for
thy speedy exercise, and ease,
thou mayest cause one to read
the Dictionarie to thee, while
thou writest it, and so in that
small quantitie of paper hast
thou to exercise thee more, then
if thou shouldest write whole
volumes. And if thou wilt take
pains to cast the Characterie
wordes into some discourse, as
liketh thee best, so hast thou a
means of reteyning the Art,
and keeping it without al dan­
ger of forgetting.

Moreouer, thou mayest ex­
ercise this art after a while lear­
ning, as well by reading with­







out writing as if thou diddest
write, by calling the Character
to thy minde, and the worde
of reference. So hast thou the
Art of short, swift, and secrete
writing, none comparable.

Farewell.








THE ARTE OF
Characterie




CHaracterie is an art
of writing brieflie.
It hath two parts.
The first parte is
concerning the making of the
Characters.

The second is concerning
the value, and signification of
them.

A Character is a brief mark
of a worde.

To Characters doe belong
two thinges: Figure, and Ioy­
ning.

The Figure ought to be ea­
sie, and briefe.

The ioyning of Characters
consisteth of situation, and di­
stinction








The situation of Character
ought to be one directly vnder
the other.

The distinction ought to be
made with a pricke sette vnder
the Character, at euery brea­
thing, or pause of the sentence
as [shorthand mark]

These are properties be­
longing to all Characters.

The kindes of Characters are
two: Simple or compound.

A Simple one is a Character
made of no other, and is vari­
ed by euery kinde of position,
hanging or lying.

It is either a straight line, or
crooked.

A straight line hanging,
direct, as, [shorthand mark] or bias as [shorthand marks] lying,
thus, [shorthand mark]







A crooked line, is either
halfe circle or whole, hanging
thus, [shorthand marks] lying thus [shorthand marks] The
whole circle thus [shorthand mark]

The Compounded Charac­
ters are such as be made of the
simple.

A Compounded Character
hath two partes: the one is the
body of the Character, &  the
other is an addition to it.

The bodie of the Character
is a single straight Character, &
& by it is varied the position of
the Character compounded
thereof.

The Addition is to either
side of the bodie, to the head
or to the foote.

The addition is either sin­
gle, or Compound.

Single, when the addition is
is of one line onely: and that ey­







ther streight, or croked.

Streight line to the head of
either side, thus: [shorthand marks] to
the foote, thus: [shorthand marks]


A compounde addition is
when the addition consisteth
of two lines.

The Compound addition is
of two sorts: either of the same
kinde of lines, as two crooked
as [shorthand mark], or one streight and the o­
ther crooked, at the head
thus [shorthand marks], at the foote thus [shorthand marks]



Hetherto of the parts of streight lined
Characters, and the properties
belonging to them.

Thus much concerning the Character, and
of the first part of Charactery.









The seconde part of Cha­
racterie, touching the sig­
nification of the
Character

THe signification of the
character is of two sorts,
Solitarie, or accom­
panied.

A solitarie signification is
that, which a solitarie Charac­
ter expresseth.

That is, certaine wordes
whereto all other may bee re­
ferred, called Charactericall.

The Charactericall wordes,
are a number that haue neither
agreement, nor contrarity to­
gether: but stand indifferent­
ly affected.

These are all conteyned in
a Table following, with the







Character adioyned to eache
word.

Let a short Character, serue
an vsuall and short word: and a
long one a long word: excepte
the order in the Alphabet dis­
posed for memorie, cause alte­
ration.

A worde of the same sound, 
though of diuers sence, is writ­
ten with the same: as, faste, for
abstinence from meate, for
swiftnes, and surenes: so if it
much differ not, as whore, and
hore, whole, and hole.



Of the properties belon­
ging to words.

THe properties belonging to
wordes, are shewed by plain
expressing them: or are gathe­
red by nature of the speech.







They are expressed by pricks
added to the side of the Cha­
racter.

They are common to all
wordes, or peculiar vnto cer­
tayne.

The common is, to be pri­
mitiue, or deriued.

Of primitiues, or
derivatiues.


PRimitiues and deriuatiues
are known by the language:
as, he is a vertuous man, not, a
vertue man: feare God, honour
the king: not fearful, so not ho­
norable.

Deriued words that ende in
er, require two prickes at the
right side of the Character: as,
laborer is deriued of labor. [shorthand mark]

Such as ende in shippe, as







friendship, or hood, a neigh­
bourhood, require after the
word writen, the Character of
ship to be placed vnderneath:
and whether it, or hood be to
be read, the language wil plain­
ly deliuer. For no man wil read
either neighborship, or friend­
hood.

Let his be written with the
Character of h, and a prick on
the left side, as: [shorthand mark]

Thus much of Primatiues and De­
riuatiues: which are common
to all wordes.



Now of peculiar
properties.

THe peculiar to Nownes are
Nomber and Comparison

Of number.

A, going before the word, de­
clareth the singular nomber:
as, a man, not, a men.







When the, goeth before, place
a pricke at the side of the Cha­
racter folowing, to note the
plurall nomber, as [shorthand mark] the ages.

The Character of this, requi­
reth a pricke on the side, to sig­
nifie thus. [shorthand mark]

The rest is declared by the
language: as, two men, not two
man.

Of comparison

The Comparative degree is
knowen from the other, by
then, following: as, gold is bet­
ter then siluer, not good then
siluer, nor best.

The superlatiue degree is de­
clared by of, following: as, gold
is best of metals.

When comparison is betwixt
two, of, signifieth the Compa­
rative degree: as, better of
twain.









Hetherto of properties belonging to
Nownes: It followeth of such
as belong to verbs, as
tence, or time.

Of Tence.

IF the time be parted from the
worde, as, I wil against such a
day, doe this: then make the
marke of the time at the side
of the person: as, at j.

The time of doing any thing,
if it be past, and is signified by
haue: let the Character for
haue, be written.

If had, doe signifie it, make
a pricke in the Character of
haue, on the left hand. [shorthand mark]

If did, make the like pricke
in the Character of doe, on the
same side. [shorthand mark]

For were, write the Charac­
ter of ware.

If the worde by reason of







tence ende in ed, as, I loued,
then make a prick in the Char­
acter of the word, on the lefte
side. [shorthand mark] I loued

The time to come requireth
a prick on the the right side.

When woulde is to bee ex­
pressed, write the Character of
will for it, and read it would: &
for should, make a prick on the
right side of well.

The present tence wanteth a
pricke, and so is knowen from
other tences.

A worde of doing, that en­
deth in ing, as, eating, drinking
&c. requireth two prickes di­
rect vnder the bodie of the
Character, as [shorthand mark]

Other times or tences, depend
vpon these, and are plainly dis­
cerned by the nature of the
language.









A note of nombers, &
propper names.

NOmbers are writen by the
the heades of the compounde
Characters, with a streight bo­
die hanging, and take increase
by place, as Ciphers in Arith­
metike:

Propper names, if they be sig­
nificant, are written by charac­
ter: as, fielde, day, &c Other­
wise the head of the character
bearing the figure of a letter
added also to the foote, and so
ioyned in one figure, may serue
for two letters: as ab [shorthand mark] ac
[shorthand mark] ad [shorthand mark]
ae [shorthand mark] ba [shorthand mark] be &c and so o­
ther two, till all the worde, or
as much as is necessarie, shal be
written: with a marke at the
side of the first Character, to
shewe that it is a name.









Hetherto concerning the
solitary signification, with properties
belonging to words. It fol­
loweth of the accompa­
nied signification

The accompanied significa­
tion is that which the Cha­
racter expresseth by an addi­
tion to it.

This addition, is the heads
of streight characters, each an­
swering a letter in the alpha­
bet.

These additions carrie the
first letter of the accompanied
signification, to declare what
it is.

If the accompanied signifi­
cation haue two wordes of like
beginning as ledde, and latine,
take two of the first letters
made into one Character, as
before in names, for difference.







if two, be like, (which is ve­
ry rare) leaue the vouell, or the
consonant, of the one, for dif­
ference, as in stretch, & straine,
& take that which may make
the difference.

The accompanied signifi­
cation is of two kinds.

Either when the very worde
is signified, or the sence only.

The very worde, when the
presise word is to be signified.

Here sometimes falleth out
a shortning of writing.

First, when and, or, neither,
or such coniunctions come be­
twixt two wordes that are of
like signification, or contraries:
then, in stead of the Character
of the second, place the addi­
tion answering the first letter
of that word, to the side of the
coniunction as [shorthand mark] life and death.







And if many synominaes, or
contraries be vttered not cou­
pled by any coniunction, make
a pricke in stead of the coniun­
ction, and note those synomi­
naes, or contraries, with their
marks, as before is said, as
part whole, lumpe, peece, thus:
[shorthand marks]

The second shortning is in
repetitions.

If the repetition be of sin­
gle wordes, the figure of the
number how oft it is repeated
is to be added to the left side
of the Character of the repea­
ted word, as earth, earth, earth,
heare the word of the Lord.

If it be of a sentence, or whole
part thereof, place a circle on
the right side of the first re­
peated woordes Character.







thus: In perils of waters, in
perils of robbers, in perils of
the sea, write thus [shorthand marks]

The accompanied significa­
tion in expresse word, is of two
sorts. Either of like, & consen­
ting signification with the soli­
tary: or of dissenting.

The consenting signification
requireth the marke on the left
side.

The consenting signification
is of two kindes.

When the accompanied sig­
nification is a synonima to the
solitary, or a kind of it.

Synonima, as anger, rage,
fury, [shorthand marks]

A kinde as mettall, brasse,
tinne, ledde. [shorthand mark]









Thus much of the Consen­
ting signification: It followeth
of the Dissenting.

The dissenting signification
is when the accompanied
dissenteth from the solitary.

Here the addition is to be
placed on the right side, as:

When it is a meere deniying
it hath onely a dashe through
the Character, whose worde it
denieth, as, is, is not; good, not
good. [shorthand marks]

Here place the shortning on
the right side, as life, & death,
neither good nor badde. [shorthand marks]

When such a contrary is first
to be written, as is not contey­
ned in the table of Charactery
wordes, then write it with the
addition, and let the coniun­







ction be accompanied with the
marke on the left side, as death
and life [shorthand mark]

Which marke is a pricke if it
be the solitary signification that
is to be written, otherwise a
marke of synonima, therof



Hetherto concerning the accompanied
signification, of the expresse word:
It followeth of the sense.

The sense onlie is to be ta­
ken with the character, when
besides that wee desire to bee
swift, the very expresse worde
is not necessarie. That is, when
they doe but fill the speech, or
otherwise are not of the sub­
stance of the matter: as, circum­
locutions, and partitions.

Of the first sort is, when we
leue out the speciall, and pecu­
liar proprieties: as, high hea­
uen, eternall God. Here leaue







out high, and eternall.

So when a phrase is to be ta­
ken with a worde: as, he tooke
himselfe to his heeles, for he
fled.

Also here may be lefte out
the word of like signification,
as praise for commend, greeue,
for molest, etc.

The other sort is when the
thing described, or diuided, is
put for the description, or di­
uision: as, for creator of heauen
and earth, God: the redeemer
of mankinde, Christ.

So, for master, seruant, chil­
dren, father, mother, olde and
young: the whole familie.

At the side of the Character
of the thing described, place
at the divided [shorthand mark]

FINIS.
