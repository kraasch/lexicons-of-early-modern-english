TO
THE MOST
HIGH AND
Mightie Prince,
IAMES,
By the grace of GOD,
King of great Brittayne,
France and Ireland,
Defender of the
Faith,&c.



THe glorious Go­
spell of Christ Ie­
sus (which is our
light, our com­
fort, the testimo­
nie of Gods fauour, and the
foode of our soules) being so
zealously embraced, so religi­







ously professed, and so mighti­
ly defended by your most ex­
cellent Maiestie, hath induced
many of your Highnesse Sub­
iects (which formerly sate in
darknesse, and in the shaddow
of death,) to make diligent
search into the holy Scriptures,
and to hunger and thirst after
the Kingdome of heauen and
the righteousnesse thereof; to
esteeme all things vile, so they
may winne Christ, and that im­
mortall weight of glorie, which
is prepared for the Elect chil­
dren and Saints of God: of this
kinde, some hauing a religious
desire, to worke their saluation
with feare and trembling; be­
ing taught that the Law of God
giueth light  and vnderstanding
to the simple, that his Word is
a Lanterne to their feete and a
light vnto their pathes, find by







reason of their ignorance, and
for that they were not brought
vp in the facultie of reading,
themselues depriued of many
vnspeakable comforts, procee­
ding from serious meditation
in the Law of God; I was ma­
ny yeares past instantly impor­
tuned by diuers religiously af­
fected, to inuent some speedie
method, whereby with small
losse of time, these obstacles
might bee remoued, and their
godly desires satisfied. To this
end (most dread Soueraigne)
I thought it necessarie to col­
lect all English words into one
small Treatise, that hauing lear­
ned onely this Booke with vn­
derstanding, they might pro­
ceede to the reading either of
the holy Scriptures, or of any
other Booke, with much facili­
tie, for that all bookes doe con­







sist of words. In that I haue
sorted them Alphabetically, it
serueth to diuers good vses, and
truely the dependance which
one word hath of another,
draweth on the Learner with
delight, and confirmeth the
memorie; and in that they are
only words, and not continued
sentences, it enforceth the lear­
ner to rely wholly vpon the
sound of the Letters. Now,
finding by long and approued
experience this order & Trea­
tise to performe my expectati­
on and premised intendment, I
haue for the more profitable
and publike vse of the same, in­
uented and annexed, briefe and
compendious Tables, contay­
ning the vse and practice of A­
rithmetike, with diuers other
things both necessarie & need­
full to be knowne.







And hauing finished this
Treatise, tending very much to
the generall good of your
Highnesse Subiects, I doe in
most humble wise (prostrate at
your Highnesse foote-stoole)
present the same to your most
excellent Maiestie, beseeching
your gracious clemency to par­
don my presumption herein.
The Lord of heauen and earth
blesse your Maiestie with much
happinesse and felicity here on
earth, and crowne you with
eternall happinesse in
the highest hea­
uens.

Your Highnesse most
loyall Subiect and
humble seruant,

IOHN EVANS.
















The Promise, and per­
formance of this
TREATISE.

1. To spell and reade euery
word distinctly, and con­
sequently, euery English booke.

2. To write euery word plain­
ly, orthographically, and truly.

3. To answere any ordinarie
Arithmeticall question without
the vse either of Pen or Coun­
ters.

4. To frame all bonds obliga­
torie with iudgement, certaine­
ty, and reason, although they ne­
uer learne any one Latine word,
more then is contayned in this
Booke.







These qualities (by this Booke
alone) are easily obtayned by the
ingenious and capable in one
moneth: And with much ease
and expedition, of all others that
are of more tender vnderstan­
ding.







All the 
letters are
either{

Vowells, a.e.i.o.u.
Consonants, b.c.d.f.
g.h.k.l.m.n.p.q.
r.s.t.w.x.y.z.
Dipthongs, ai. ea.
ee. ei. oo.

A vowell is a letter, which
maketh a full & perfect sound of
himselfe, as, a, man, I, am.

A consonant is a letter which
must needes bee sounded with a
vowell, as ba, ca, da, fa, &c.

A Dipthonge is the sound of
two vowells in one sillable, as in
this word, said.

Vowells.

1. Note, that there can be no
sillable without a vowell.

2. Note, that you must di­
uide euery word into so many
sillables as there bee vowells in
it, as, sa-lu-ta-ti-on.

3. Note, that when a vowell
beginneth a word, and a single
consonant followeth, you must
spell that vowell alone, as, A­
dam, A-bide.







4. Note, that when you find
a vowell, and two or more con­
sonants following, you must
ioyne that vowell and the first
consonant together, as in ab­
sent, ab-so-lu-ti-on.

5. Note, that E in the end of 
a word, serueth onely to cause
that word to bee pronounced
long: as in blame, name, pate,
hate.

Consonants.

1. Note, that when a Con­
sonant beginneth a word, and
after the vowell a single conso­
nant followeth, you must make
vp your sillable with that vow­
ell, as in ge-ne-ra-ti-on, re-com­
men-da-ti-on.

2. So that a consonant placed
betwixt two vowels, is ioyned
in spelling to the latter, as in,
ge-ne-ro-si-tie.

3. But if a vowell be placed
betwixt two consonants, you
must take one of the consonants
to the first sillable, and the rest
left for the latter sillable, as in,







constitution, configuration, con­
ten-ta-ti-on.

4. Note, that I. and V. albeit
they are vowels, yet comming
before any vowell, are changed in­
to consonants, as in Iesus, Ieru­
salem, va-nitie.

5. Note, that x is alwayes ioy­
ned to the precedent vowell, as in
these, ex-tremitie, ex-ercise.

6. Note, that c before e or i,
is sounded like s, as in ceremonie,
cir-cumstance.

7. Note, that ph beareth the 
sound of f, and ps of s, as in these,
Pharisie, Philemon, Psalmes,
Psalter.

8. Note, that ti before an o,
spelleth si, as in contention.

9. Note, that ch, sh, sl, st, are
compound consonants, and must
neuer be sundered, as in these, in­
chantment, cherishing, shamefast­
nesse, shifting, slander, standard.

Dipthongs, ai. ea. ee. ei. oo.

1. Note, that where you finde
any of these Dipthongs, you must







not part them, but the sound of
the sillable shall more incline to
the first vowell than to the latter
as in these words, Maide, brain,
faile, heauen, earth, great, health,
feede, neede, bleede; either, nei­
ther; good, goodnesse, stood, food,
foote.

These Rules being well taught
and conceiued of the Learner, will
bring much profite and delight, for
that the difficultie of reading chief
ly consisteth in the ill sorting of
their sillables, which by these rules
they may easily auoid: but for as
much as all things are better vn­
derstood by example than precept
I haue collected all English words
into this Volume and parted them
into sillables for the ease and helpe
of the Learner.
